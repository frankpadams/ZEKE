# ZEKE Evidence Library — v0.6.0 Alpha

ZEKE discoveries and coaching prompts should distinguish personal observations from empirical research. This release embeds a small starter evidence library for demonstration.

## Resistance-training progression

American College of Sports Medicine. Progression Models in Resistance Training for Healthy Adults. *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*. 2009.

https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19204579/

ZEKE use: conservative load-progression context, including the guideline that load can be increased when the current workload can be performed for additional repetitions beyond the target range.

## Resistance-training prescription and outcomes

Currier BS, et al. Resistance training prescription for muscle strength and hypertrophy in healthy adults: a systematic review and Bayesian network meta-analysis. *British Journal of Sports Medicine*. 2023.

https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10579494/

ZEKE use: context for the distinction between training prescriptions that prioritize strength versus hypertrophy. ZEKE should not treat one prescription as universally optimal for every user.

## Family history and cardiovascular risk context

Family history research used in the alpha discovery context:

https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7256470/

ZEKE use: family cardiovascular history may provide relevant risk context. It must not be treated as a diagnosis or a deterministic prediction.

## ADHD and suicidality research context

Population-level research used only as cautious contextual evidence:

https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11538115/

ZEKE use: personal or family psychiatric history can inform questions and context, but ZEKE must not infer current symptoms, diagnosis, or individual risk from history alone.

## Evidence behavior rule

A ZEKE evidence view should make clear:

1. what was observed in the user's data;
2. what is interpretation;
3. what external research says;
4. where the research does or does not generalize to the user;
5. confidence and limitations;
6. what additional data would improve the conclusion.
